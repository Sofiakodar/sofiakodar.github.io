I’ve been a manager for ~11 years now and was a team lead for a few years before that. Two of the most important leader responsibilities in my opinion is setting clear expectations and giving feedback. 

In all my years as manager I have only received training in giving feedback once (when I found a training and convinced my manager that the whole leadership team should take it). I've read and learned by myself and by experience. I can’t count how many times I’ve seen other managers struggle with giving feedback and asking for more skills in how to give feedback. When I’ve mentored new Engineering managers and team leads, this is the main question and the area they struggle the most with. I’ve also time and again seen the consequences of missing feedback and how one person’s behavior can sink a whole team. 

This blog post is probably the first in a series about feedback, with a few of my tips and tricks. 

### Do you really care?
I find that there’s a huge difference between wanting to give feedback because you’re annoyed with someone and want to put them into place, or wanting to give feedback because you genuinely care and want to help that person improve. The best feedback comes from a place of caring and helps the other person grow. 

I care a lot about my colleagues. I’ve had to give quite tough feedback several times where the person I’ve talked to have told me that they can take it, because they know that I care and will help them grow. 

I once had to give feedback to a developer who wasn’t performing up to expectations but had no idea. His team lead had been unhappy for quite some time but hadn’t mustered the courage to let him know and instead became bitter and irritated towards him. When I explained the situation to the developer and what was missing, he was very surprised. After thinking it over for a day or two, he came back to me and was grateful to finally understand why things had been tense between him and the team lead and super motivated to improve. 

Another time I had a developer who applied to a role as team lead, but didn’t get it. I sat down with him and explained what he was lacking and what he could improve and even though he was very disappointed, he was able to turn that into motivation to improve on those points to be able to take that role later on. I had to think things through before the conversation to put the reason he was rejected into very clear points that he could take action on. 

Would it have been easier to just avoid those tough conversations? Absolutely. But then none of them would have gotten the chance to grow and improve.

### How to give feedback
I strongly believe that there always is a reason for someone’s behavior – it might be a stupid reason, but no one acts bad without an underlying reason. I like the Non-violent communication framework and attitude. I know that many people only think of it as a communication format, but for me it’s more of an attitude towards others. 

One of the main things in NVC is to always talk about the observation, instead of the evaluation/judgement you have made of the situation. 
“She is lazy” vs “She is sitting on the sofa reading instead of working”. 
“He is always late to meetings” vs “He has been late to three meetings this week”. 
“He doesn’t care about the team" vs "He was looking out the window and not engaging in the conversation” (Maybe he was solving the problem in his head and deeply engaged?). 

Without going into too many details about NVC, this is a well-known method in conflict management and couple’s therapy. The format is: Observation. Feeling. Needs. Request. Example: 
"When you were 10 minutes late to the last three team meetings (Observation), I was frustrated (Feeling) because I need efficiency and respect for our time (Need). Would you be willing to arrive on time next week? (Request)".

I won't go through the details in this blog post but: Stay true to the facts, and avoid judgements and especially labels (“You’re lazy”, “You don’t care”). 
Compare: “You’re always late, you’re so disrespectful. You don’t care about your team’s productivity at all!” 
With the better approach: “You have been late to our team meetings three times in a row now, this affects the whole team. Is there a reason it’s difficult for you to be on time? Could you please be on time in the future?“

So, state the objective facts, explain the consequences and state your request clearly. I would also add to be open to hearing the real reason for the behavior and find a solution together. 
This is very similar to Situation-Behavior-Impact (SBI): 
- Note the time and place in which a behavior occurred. 
- Describe the behavior, what you saw and heard. 
- Describe the impact the behavior had in terms of the your thoughts, feelings or actions.

It’s a quite simple format, but I find that I more often see managers and colleagues avoid saying anything to the person, and instead talk behind the person's back and get more and more frustrated. Talk to them instead! With respect of course. 

“Last Friday when the whole team was struggling with an urgent incident, you packed up and left early without offering to stay and help. This made me irritated. For me it’s important that we share the responsibility in the team. Could you tell me your view of this situation?” <br> 
Maybe your colleague had a very important personal obligation that would make your irritation disappear. Maybe they were oblivious to the expectations in the team? Aren’t you curious to find out? 

Asking from a place of caring and with curiosity can usually do wonders.

### When to give feedback
For constructive feedback, I would recommend as soon as possible. In the example above, Monday morning would be much better that walk around annoyed at the person for weeks and giving them a sour comments about how they don’t care about the team. 

There have been many times where I’ve grabbed someone immediately after a meeting or situation. Waiting for 1-2 weeks or until the next 1-1 is too long. The only reason to wait a bit is if you or the other person are upset and can’t have a calm and productive discussion. 

Always make it clear that you will give feedback. Unless you are the person’s manager and the feedback is urgent, say “I have some feedback I would like to give to you. Are you in a state where you can receive it now?”. The person could have a terrible day and not be in a state to get constructive feedback. 
Don’t trick them with a “Do you have five minutes for a quick chat?” – that just creates paranoida for all future ad-hoc discussions. Always be clear on what it is about. 

I find that explaining why I’m giving the feedback, showing empathy and also assume good intent helps. If you care, assume good intent and are curious to find out the other person's view – you can solve really difficult situations. I've found it super helpful. Someone who is an underperformer could, in my experience, be: 
- completely stuck and panicked and not know what to do 
- be harassed by his team lead and not dare to ask for help 
- feel underappreciated and not even know that people are waiting for their tasks
- have a terrible time in their personal life and having trouble focusing at work 
- be completely oblivious to the expectations and have no idea that they're underperforming
- be very unhappy at work but not been honest with themselves and should look for something else

### Who should you give feedback to?
If it’s positive feedback and encouragement, you should give it to anyone. Your peers, your boss, your team mate, other colleagues. I believe that we all want to be appreciated and noticed. If someone holds a great presentation at an All-hands, I often send a quick slack message afterwards giving them praise and specific points that were good. Even if praise can be uplifting, it will be more valuable if you can point ut some concrete things that made it good (“You made your point very clearly. You had the right amount of slides and great jokes. Your call to action was very motivating”). 

When it comes to constructive feedback: do you have a basic level of trust with that person so there’s a chance they will take it to heart? If you are the person’s manager however, it’s always your responsibility. 
Don’t forget to give feedback to your manager as well, most managers get too little feedback. 

There’s so much more to write about feedback. I’ve so far not found a good book that covers feedback specifically, to recommend. For me it has been lots of difference sources and learnings over the years.
