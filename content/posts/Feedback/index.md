I’ve been a manager for ~11 years now and team lead for a few years before that. Two of the most important leader responsibilities in my opinion is setting clear expectations and giving feedback. 

In all my years as manager I have only received training in giving feedback once (I found a training and convinced my manager that the whole leadership team should take it). I've read and learned by myself and by experience. I can’t count how many times I’ve seen other managers struggle with giving feedback and asking for more skills in how to give feedback. When I’ve mentored new Engineering managers and team leads, this is the main question and the area they struggle the most with. I’ve also time and again seen the consequences of missing feedback and how one person’s behavior can sink a whole team. Here are some of my tips and thoughts. 

### Different kinds of feedback
When I say "feedback" most people think of negative or "constructive" feedback. I think it's important to give positive feedback when someone is doing something right. Everyone deserves to feel appreciated at work and life. 

Most people try to do the right thing, but without any guidance it's easy to put the effort into the wrong areas. If someone always gets praise when they are heroes and handle urgent issues, they might not spend time on solving the underlying issues that causes them. I've seen junior engineers get praise for organizing meetings and social stuff, but not for their actual coding. That can make them start doing that more instead and not continue to hone their engineering skills. 

### Do you really care?
I find that there’s a huge difference between wanting to give feedback because you’re annoyed with someone and want to put them into place, or wanting to give feedback because you genuinely care and want to help that person. The best feedback comes from a place of caring and helps the other person grow. It's much easier to listen to feedback when you know that the person cares about you. 

I once had to give feedback to a developer who wasn’t performing up to expectations but had no idea. His team lead had been unhappy for quite some time but hadn’t mustered the courage to let him know and instead became bitter and irritated towards him. When I explained the situation to the developer and what was missing, he was very surprised. After thinking it over for a day or two, he came back to me and was grateful to finally understand why things had been tense between him and the team lead and super motivated to improve. 

Another time I had a developer who applied to a role as team lead, but didn’t get it. I sat down with him and explained what he was lacking and what he could improve and even though he was very disappointed, he was able to turn that into motivation to improve on those points to be able to take that role later on. I had to think things through before the conversation to put the reason he was rejected into very clear points that he could take action on, and how I could support him in growing into it. 

Would it have been easier to just avoid those tough conversations? Absolutely. But then none of them would have gotten the chance to grow and improve.

### How to give feedback
I strongly believe that there always is a reason for someone’s behavior – it might be a stupid reason, but there is always a reason. If you care about the person and are curious to find out why, I think there's a higher chance of succeeding. 

I like the Non-Violent Communication (NVC) framework. For me it's much more than a communication format, it’s an attitude towards others. One of the main things in NVC is to always use a neutral observation, instead of an evaluation/judgement of the situation. 
*“She is always late to meetings”* vs *"She has been late to three meetings this week”*. 
*“He doesn’t care about the team"* vs *"He was looking out the window and not engaging in the conversation”* (Maybe he was solving the problem in his head and deeply engaged?). 

NVC is a well-known method in conflict management. The format is: Observation. Feeling. Needs. Request.  It's much more than that, but for this blog post the TLDR is: State the objective facts, avoid judgements and labels. Explain the consequences and state your request clearly. 

Compare: *“You’re always late, you’re so disrespectful. You don’t care about your team’s productivity at all!”* 
<br>With the better approach: “You have been late to our team meetings three times in a row now (*observation*), this disrupts the meeting and makes me frustrated (*feeling*). It's important for me that we respect each other's time (*need*). Could you please be on time in the future? (*request*)“

Another popular format for feedback is Situation-Behavior-Impact (SBI): 
- Note the time and place in which a behavior occurred. 
- Describe the behavior, what you saw and heard. 
- Describe the impact the behavior had in terms of your thoughts, feelings or actions.

Both can of course also be used for positive feedback: *"When I saw you spend time teaching our new colleague about our product and patiently walked through our roadmap yesterday, it made me very happy. It's important for me that we make our new colleagues feel welcome and included. Thank you."*

It’s a quite simple format, so try it out. I often see managers and colleagues avoid saying anything to the person, and instead talk behind the person's back and get more and more frustrated. Talk to them instead! With respect of course. Asking from a place of caring and with curiosity can do wonders. 

*“Last Friday when the whole team was struggling with an urgent incident, you packed up and left early without offering to stay and help. This put all the work on your remaining two colleagues and made me stressed. For me it’s important that we share the responsibility in the team. Could you tell me your view of this situation?”* <br> 
Maybe your colleague had a very important personal obligation that would make your irritation disappear. Maybe they were oblivious to the expectations in the team? Aren’t you curious to find out?

### How to _not_ give feedback
Don't give second hand feedback unless absolutely necessary. *"X said that you..." "I heard that you..."*. Second hand feedback is usually terrible, you don't have all the information and it quickly becomes a "They said..." situation. There is a much higher risk of you getting into a discussion about what *really* happened, than for any feedback to take root. No one likes hearing that people have been talking behind their back. 
<br>If possible, always give feedback from your point of view and what *you* have observed. If something is happening in a team, make sure that you're there and can see it yourself so you can give real feedback.  

### Who should you give feedback to?
If it’s positive feedback and encouragement, you should give it to anyone and everyone. Your peers, your boss, your team mate, other colleagues. I believe that we all want to be appreciated and noticed. If someone holds a great presentation at an All-hands, I often send a quick slack message afterwards giving them praise and specific points that were good. Even if praise can be uplifting, it will be more valuable if you can point ut some concrete things that made it good (“You made your point very clearly. You had the right amount of slides and great jokes. Your call to action was very motivating”). 

If it's constructive feedback and you're *not* their team lead or manager: Do you have a basic level of trust with that person so there’s a chance they will take it to heart? Are you sure that you are right about the feedback? Sometimes feedback says more about the person giving it, than the person recieving it. Don’t forget to give feedback to your manager as well, most managers get too little feedback. 
<br>If you are the person’s manager and the behaviour is causing consequences, it’s *always* your responsibility.

### When to give feedback
I would recommend as soon as possible. There have been many times where I’ve grabbed someone immediately after a meeting or situation. Waiting for 1-2 weeks or until the next 1-1 is too long. This is true for positive feedback as well, while the person remembers what they were doing and can reflect on it. The only reason to wait a bit is if you or the other person are upset and can’t have a calm and productive discussion. 

Always make it clear that you will give feedback. Unless the feedback is urgent, say “I have some feedback I would like to give to you. Can I do it now?”. The person could have a terrible day and not be in a state to get constructive feedback. 
Don’t trick them with a “Do you have five minutes for a quick chat?” – that just creates paranoida for all future ad-hoc discussions. Always be clear on what it is about. 

If you care, assume good intent and are curious to find out the other person's view – you can solve really difficult situations. 

Someone who is an underperformer could, in my experience: 
- be completely stuck and panicked and not know what to do 
- be harassed by their team lead and not dare to ask for help 
- feel underappreciated and not even know that people are waiting for their tasks
- have a terrible time in their personal life and having trouble focusing at work 
- be completely oblivious to the expectations and have no idea that they're underperforming
- be very unhappy at work but not been honest with themselves and should look for something else
